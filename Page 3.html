Valley of Ashes

----- The environment within the valley of ashes where Tom and Nick approaches George Wilson with his wife Myrtle, gives a personal background to the morally corrupted environment. Geroge Wilson is described as an “Spiritless” (pg. 25) man, appearing like the anemic “ash-gray men” (pg. 23), roaming though the “powdery air” (pg. 23) of the valley of ashes, an indication of the consumption of humanity’s own social rules in the upbringings of human desire. Fitzgerald’s comparison of the symbolical nature of a morally deceased environment, is presented as a heavy impact on Geroge Wilson’s life, dedicated to the debt of wealth. When Myrtle, the cheating wife, transfers to her and Tom’s secret nest of love, others like Nick and Catherine are invited during their intimate session. Catherine intoxicates Nick into the chaos, making the bold statement that it was his “Second time getting drunk” (pg. 29), trapping Nick with the immoral atmosphere that eventually leads to a paradox of Nick looking at himself “in another life” (pg. 30). Nick experiences beginning signs of moral corruption from how a significantly miniature community yet still manages to be unreasonably haywire under such a tight space. The paradoxical version of Nick views up at the unjustified behaviors of the party, with Tom Buchanan punching Myrtle in the nose after repeating Daisy’s name while others retreat to their individual wealth, as if nothing happened.

Gatsby’s Reunion with Daisy

----- The next significant detail in which Jordon comes back from having a private conversation with Gatsby, as Nick is eventually told about Gatsby motives for Daisy in one of the parties, wanting him to invite her over for tea without informing that Gatsby would be there. In concentrated preparation for Daisy Buchanan, Gatsby sends people at Nick’s cottage to cut the yard, while also attempting to give Nick a job as Gatsby remarkes that he “don’t make much money” (pg. 82), wanting to pay him back for the reunion. Gatsby checks up on Nick as the servants overloaded the Cottage with flowers and decoration, with the Jay Gatsby having sighs of “sleeplessness” (pg. 84) according to Nicks interpretation. Based on Gatsby’s restlessness, Fitzgerald finally reveals the meaning of the greenlight directed at Gatsby’s vision of the American dream, indicating that the only reason for Gatsby’s wealth is to impress Daisy Buchanan. This also correlates to humanity offering gratitude towards exchanging wealth to what they desire most dearly, diluting the mind of themselves to follow under the path of wealth regarding the trial they fight against.
