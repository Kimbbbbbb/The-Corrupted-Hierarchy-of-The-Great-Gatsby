Introduction

----- With F. Scott Fitzgerald’s modernist novel, The Great Gatsby, Nick Carraway, the narrator of the novel, travels after the war from the Midwest to a small house at Long Island, advocating his and others’ criticism of the social occasions that he’s laid on based on his experience within the West egg. Fitzgerald expresses the behavior of the 1920s jazz age of America as carelessness beneath the chaotic nature of Gatsby’s’ parties and other scenarios where corruption overtakes. Through these reckless settings designed to present wealth, Fitzgerald argues the moral decay sprouting from the hypocritical illusion of the American dream against the reality of corruption beneath the glamour.

Daisy x Tom

----- After Nick Carraway moves to Long Island in West Egg, he is invited to have dinner with the Buchanan family along East egg. The appearance of wealth formed throughout the house as Tom brings Nick in to see. At the dinner table, Tom conversates that he is “stronger and more of a man” (pg. 7) than Nick, panning to Daisy talking to him about how she would want her child to be a “beautiful little fool” (pg. 17). Fitz Gerald creates a confusing scenario between the Buchanan’s with contrasting parallels of Tom’s belief of the ability to dominate over anyone depending on wealth, while Daisy believes to marry a man with high status, applying gender roles set for the social standard in the 1920s. When Jordan is introduced to Nick, she tells him the issues between the relationship of Daisy and Tom, but Nick mentions to readers of “unpleasant story” (pg. 18) he heard about her, appearing “motionless” (pg. 19) of her composure of life. Fitgerald elaborates the oblivious Daisy and the directly independent Jordan Baker, yet habits superficiality of society.
